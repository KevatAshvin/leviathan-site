## Why context beats raw Seller Central screenshots

Seller Central snapshots without narrative context force auditors to reverse-engineer your business blind. Were you migrating from **MFN to FBA** mid-quarter? Launching bulky kitchen sets with unconventional cartonization? Running Vine reviews that spike returns temporarily? Those facts change how we weigh inbound shortages versus customer-damaged classifications.

Spend thirty minutes documenting **commercial reality**: average selling price bands, sourcing regions, fulfillment partners, return rates by ASIN cluster, promotional calendar spikes, and any prior reimbursement tickets. If you attempted self-filing in the past, include Amazon case IDs—even denied cases help us anticipate evidentiary gaps.

Understanding **cash timing** matters too: settlement cycles, PPC draws, inbound placement fees, and remote fulfillment surcharges alter how imbalances appear versus what actually transpired physically. Preparation is not bureaucracy; it is **signal sharpening**.

## Mandatory exports and dashboards to gather

While every auditor’s toolkit differs, the following bundles accelerate almost every Leviathan Sellers engagement:

**Inventory narratives**

- Detailed **Inventory Ledger** for the suspected window (extend one month backward for trend context).
- **Reserved inventory breakdown** snapshots for peak ASINs to separate customer orders versus FC transfers versus investigations.

**Fulfillment lineage**

- FBA inbound plans with shipment IDs, label PDFs where available, carton content manifests, pallet photos, carrier BOL signatures.
- Removal order logs if liquidations coincided with variances—you would be shocked how frequently removals collide with disputed lost units.

**Money trails**

- **Settlement reports**, transaction views, fee preview downloads.
- Lightweight tie-outs from your bookkeeping system (cost of goods, landed freight accruals) so we can cross-check reimbursement expectations.

## Stakeholders you should invite to the kickoff

Modern Amazon accounts resemble matrix organizations. Loop in whoever touches:

- **Inbound coordination** — third-party prep centers or internal dock teams.
- **Finance & tax** — especially if invoices must be sanitized for nondisclosure.
- **Advertising & promotion** — large coupons can inflate return ratios and skew interpretation temporarily.
- **Customer service** — return reason codes materially influence reimbursements classifications.

Establish a single Slack or Teams channel pinned with **immutable evidence folders** dated by week. Naming conventions like `Inbound_20260312_EWR4` outperform “stuff.zip.” Your future auditors will silently thank you.

## Data hygiene pitfalls that undermine preparation

Incomplete preparation usually fails in predictable ways:

- **Stale COGS spreadsheets** referencing 2023 quotes when 2026 BOM costs shifted after resin price spikes—Amazon’s manufacturing-cost orientation magnifies discrepancies between “what you wish you paid” and “what invoices prove.”
- **ZIP archives missing hash integrity** causing delayed evidence acceptance during appeals.
- **Mixed marketplace reporting** accidentally blending Amazon.ca with Amazon.com settlement IDs.

Run a reconciliation triage worksheet before external review:

| Check | Intended outcome |
| --- | --- |
| COGS freshness | Verified invoice matches each hero ASIN |
| Inbound lineage | Closed loop from factory carton to FC receipt |
| Return reasons | Mapped to plausible disposition outcomes |
| Dimensional audits | SKU master matches physical samples |
| Policy calendar | Claims bucketed inside **2025-era deadlines** |

If any row surfaces crimson cells, remediate internally first or annotate limitations transparently—we would rather reschedule a week than file weak packets.
