In 2026 it controls your storage capacity, your restock headroom, your fee exposure, and your ability to scale into Q4 — and Amazon has made it clear they are not loosening their grip.

## What changed in 2026: monthly, cubic-feet-based capacity

The biggest shift is structural. As of January 2026, Amazon moved from its longstanding **quarterly** storage-limit system to a **dynamic monthly capacity model**. Capacity is no longer static or purely volume-based — it is recalculated every month from performance metrics and operational forecasting.

Three things every seller needs to internalize:

- **Capacity is measured in cubic feet, not units.** Your limit reflects volume, so bulky SKUs consume allocation far faster than small ones.
- **Allocation is monthly and forward-looking.** Your limit is tied to projected sales over roughly the next five months — Amazon shortened this from six months in May 2025, which is when many sellers lost 40 to 75 percent of their storage almost overnight.
- **Warehouse availability is now a factor you don't control.** Even strong accounts (IPI above 550) were hit during the 2025 reduction. Individual performance improves your relative position; it does not exempt you from system-wide cuts.

The strategic takeaway: in 2026, Amazon is actively pushing sellers toward **faster inventory turns and less warehousing behavior** inside FBA. Your IPI score is the summary signal of whether you are playing by those rules.

## The 400 threshold — and why it's the floor, not the goal

Amazon's minimum IPI threshold sits at **400**, reduced from 450 in prior years. The number itself is simple; the enforcement is what changed. **Dropping below 400 now triggers restrictions almost immediately** rather than at a quarterly checkpoint. Fall below at the wrong moment — say, while ramping for Prime Day or Q4 — and you can see capacity cuts within days.

So treat the score in three bands:

- **Below 400 — danger.** Storage limits, overage charges on excess stored inventory, and potential ASIN-level restock caps.
- **400 to 549 — surviving.** You're compliant, but you have little buffer when warehouse availability tightens.
- **550 to 600-plus — operating zone.** This is where well-run accounts live. It earns better relative positioning when Amazon allocates capacity and protects restock headroom into peak season.

The mistake we see most often is sellers celebrating a 410 and assuming they're safe. A 410 is one slow-selling shipment away from a problem.

## The four IPI sub-metrics — and which to fix first

IPI is driven by four components. Knowing which one is slipping tells you exactly where to act:

- **Excess inventory percentage** — too much capital and cubic footage tied up in slow movers.
- **Stranded inventory rate** — units sitting in Amazon's warehouse with no active listing. Unsellable, but you're still paying to store them.
- **In-stock rate** — whether your replenishable ASINs stay available.
- **Sell-through rate** — how fast inventory moves relative to what you hold.

**Fix order matters.** The two fastest wins are the two you control directly today:

1. **Resolve stranded inventory immediately.** These units are pure dead weight. Amazon's documentation expects stranded status resolved within roughly **30 days** or it may initiate automated removals. Reattaching listings or removing the units is the single cleanest IPI improvement available.
2. **Clear excess and aging inventory before the surcharge hits.** Units aging **181 to 270 days** (and worse, 271 to 365) attract surcharge fees on top of standard storage. If an ASIN has been sitting six months, Amazon is actively charging you to keep it. Discount, bundle, or remove before those thresholds.

Then protect **in-stock rate** on hero ASINs with disciplined replenishment, and **sell-through** improves as a side effect of the first two. Recovery from a sub-400 score typically takes **six to ten weeks**, which is exactly why you act before a measurement period closes — not after the restriction lands.

## ASIN-level restock caps are back with teeth

A 2026 trap that catches even healthy-looking accounts: **restock limits now apply at the ASIN level**. Your overall account can look acceptable while an individual SKU gets throttled because your broader catalog health looks weak. That means your single best-selling ASIN can get restock-capped because of stranded units or excess inventory **elsewhere** in your catalog.

The lesson: IPI is a portfolio metric. A few neglected SKUs dragging down account-wide health can quietly cap the products actually paying your bills. Auditing your catalog SKU-by-SKU — not just your headline score — is how you catch this before it costs a peak-season sale.

## The Q4 over-ordering trap

The most expensive seasonal mistake is predictable and avoidable. Sellers over-order to prepare for Q4, then **carry excess into Q1** when velocity drops and storage costs are at their highest. Units that don't sell through by January attract long-term storage surcharges on top of standard fees — and the excess simultaneously drags your IPI down right when you want headroom for the next cycle.

A disciplined 2026 restock strategy looks different:

- **Ship more frequently in smaller batches** rather than front-loading one giant Q4 shipment that eats your cubic-feet allocation.
- **Forecast against the 5-month window** Amazon actually uses, not a gut feeling about peak demand.
- **Use AWD or upstream storage** for buffer stock so your FBA capacity stays reserved for fast-moving units.
- **Plan your Q1 exit before Q4 even starts** — know which units you'll discount or remove if they don't clear by January.

## Where reconciliation quietly protects your IPI

Here's a connection most sellers miss: **inventory accuracy and IPI are linked.** A unit flagged as "excess" or slow-moving might actually be **lost or uncredited inventory** — stock Amazon misplaced, damaged, or never reimbursed — masquerading as a performance problem in your numbers. Acting on bad data means you discount or remove units you should be recovering instead.

Telling those apart requires correlating your inventory health with your settlement and reimbursement state — which is exactly why we run **[reconciliation and reimbursement](/reconciliation)** together rather than as separate scans. Clean books make your IPI levers trustworthy; messy books make you optimize against phantom problems.

## Common IPI and restock mistakes that cost sellers in 2026

- **Treating 400 as a target** instead of a danger line, leaving zero buffer for warehouse-wide cuts.
- **Ignoring stranded inventory** until Amazon auto-removes it — forfeiting recoverable units and dragging IPI down for weeks.
- **Front-loading one massive Q4 shipment** that consumes your cubic-feet allocation and strands excess into Q1 surcharge territory.
- **Watching only the headline IPI** while individual ASINs get restock-capped from catalog-wide weakness.
- **Acting on inventory data without reconciling it** — discounting or removing units that are actually lost-and-recoverable.
